# Australia and New Zealand

Australian and New Zealand genealogical records benefit from strong digitization programs and well-organized state and territory-based systems. Australia's convict transportation records (1788 to 1868) are a unique and exceptionally detailed source with no parallel in other countries. Both countries have free national newspaper archives (Trove and Papers Past) that are among the best in the world.

## Key Databases (Australia)

### Trove
- **URL**: https://trove.nla.gov.au/
- **Coverage**: National Library of Australia's digitized newspaper archive (1803 onward), plus books, images, maps, music, and archived websites. Over 35 million pages of newspapers with full-text OCR search.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (full-text search across all digitized content)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Results are structured with snippets and metadata.
- **Note**: One of the best free newspaper archives in the world. Invaluable for finding obituaries, birth/marriage/death notices, court reports, immigration arrivals, and local news mentions.

### National Archives of Australia (NAA)
- **URL**: https://www.naa.gov.au/
- **Coverage**: Federal government records including immigration and passenger records, naturalization files, military service records (WWI and WWII), and security files. The RecordSearch database indexes millions of items.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (RecordSearch database)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable. Catalog is structured; many records are digitized images.

### Ancestry.com.au
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com.au/
- **Coverage**: Australian collections including convict records, immigration and passenger lists, vital records indexes, electoral rolls, military records, and newspapers.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### FamilySearch (Australia collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Indexed and image collections including church records, civil registration, and immigration records.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

### State BDM Registries
Each Australian state and territory has its own Births, Deaths, and Marriages registry. Historical indexes are increasingly available online:

- **New South Wales**: https://www.bdm.nsw.gov.au/ (indexes from 1788)
- **Victoria**: https://www.bdm.vic.gov.au/ (indexes from 1836)
- **Queensland**: https://www.bdm.qld.gov.au/ (from 1829)
- **South Australia**: https://www.sa.gov.au/topics/family-and-community/births-deaths-and-marriages
- **Western Australia**: https://www.wa.gov.au/organisation/department-of-justice/births-deaths-and-marriages
- **Tasmania**: https://www.justice.tas.gov.au/bdm (from 1803)

Index searches are generally free. Ordering certificates costs a fee (varies by state, typically AUD $30 to $60). Historical certificates contain more detail than the index entries and are worth ordering for key ancestors.

### LINC Tasmania
- **URL**: https://www.linc.tas.gov.au/
- **Coverage**: Tasmania's archives including convict records, pioneer records, names indexes, and historical photographs. Tasmania received convicts from 1803 to 1853 and has particularly rich convict documentation.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (names index and catalog)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Convict Records
- **URL**: https://convictrecords.com.au/
- **Coverage**: Database of over 160,000 convicts transported to Australia from 1788 to 1868. Searchable by name, ship, and year.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, ship, year of arrival)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Structured results.

## Key Databases (New Zealand)

### BDM Online
- **URL**: https://www.bdmonline.dia.govt.nz/
- **Coverage**: Births (1848 onward), marriages (1854 onward), deaths (1848 onward). Free index search; certificates available for purchase.
- **Cost**: Free index search. Certificates cost NZD $33.
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date range, registration number)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable for index entries.

### Archives New Zealand
- **URL**: https://www.archives.govt.nz/
- **Coverage**: Government records including immigration and passenger lists, military service records, naturalization files, and land records.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (Archway catalog)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable. Catalog is structured; many records are digitized images.

### Papers Past
- **URL**: https://paperspast.natlib.govt.nz/
- **Coverage**: Digitized New Zealand newspapers (1839 onward), periodicals, letters, diaries, parliamentary papers, and gazettes. Full-text OCR search.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (full-text search)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Results are structured with snippets and metadata.
- **Note**: The New Zealand equivalent of Trove. Excellent for obituaries, shipping news, court reports, and family notices.

### FamilySearch (New Zealand collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Church records, civil registration indexes, and other collections.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

## Convict Records (Australia)

Transportation of convicts from Britain and Ireland to Australia occurred from 1788 to 1868. Approximately 162,000 convicts were transported, primarily to New South Wales (1788 to 1840), Tasmania (then Van Diemen's Land, 1803 to 1853), and Western Australia (1850 to 1868). Victoria, South Australia, and Queensland did not receive convicts directly (or only briefly).

Convict records are exceptionally detailed and often more informative than free settler records from the same period. Key record types:

| Record Type | What It Contains |
|---|---|
| Trial records | Crime, verdict, sentence, court location, date. Held at British archives (TNA, Old Bailey Online). |
| Transportation lists | Name, crime, sentence, ship, departure and arrival dates. |
| Convict indents | Physical descriptions (height, hair, eye color, distinguishing marks), trade/occupation, literacy, religion. |
| Assignment records | Name of the settler or government department to whom the convict was assigned for labor. |
| Musters and censuses | Periodic counts of the convict population with location and status. |
| Tickets of leave | Granted to convicts for good behavior; allowed them to work for themselves within a defined district. |
| Conditional and absolute pardons | Records of pardons granting partial or full freedom. |
| Certificates of freedom | Issued when the sentence expired. |

These records collectively can reconstruct a convict ancestor's life in remarkable detail: their appearance, skills, crime, voyage, assignments, behavior, and eventual freedom.

## Immigration Records (Australia)

### Assisted Immigration
Many settlers in the 1800s received government-assisted passage to Australia. Assisted immigration applications are a rich source because applicants had to provide detailed family information, occupations, and references. These records are held by state archives.

### Unassisted Immigration
Passenger lists for unassisted immigrants survive from the 1850s onward. Coverage varies by port and period. Ship arrivals were reported in colonial newspapers (searchable on Trove).

### Post-WWII Migration
Australia received significant waves of migration after WWII: first from Southern and Eastern Europe (Italy, Greece, the former Yugoslavia, Poland), then from the Middle East, and later from East and Southeast Asia. The NAA holds immigration case files and passenger records for this period. "Displaced persons" records from the late 1940s and 1950s are particularly detailed.

## Electoral Rolls (Australia)

Mandatory voting (introduced federally in 1924, with some states earlier) makes Australian electoral rolls uniquely complete. Historical rolls (from 1903) list name, address, and occupation for virtually every adult citizen. They provide a near-complete snapshot of the adult population at regular intervals.

Electoral rolls are available on Ancestry.com.au. They are particularly useful for tracking families between census years and for finding women (who appear under their own names as voters).

## Indigenous Records

### Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander (Australia)
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander genealogical records are complex, sensitive, and fragmented across multiple agencies. Key sources:

- **Protector records**: Government "protectors of Aborigines" kept registers of Aboriginal people under their jurisdiction. Held at state archives and the NAA.
- **Mission records**: Church missions maintained registers of residents. Access varies; some are held by churches, others by state archives.
- **Stolen Generations records**: The forcible removal of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children from their families (approximately 1910 to 1970) created records held by state welfare departments, churches, and institutions. These records are sensitive and access may be restricted.
- **Link-Up**: https://www.linkupnsw.org.au/ assists Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people with family tracing and reunion services.

Researchers should be aware that these records were created by colonial and government authorities, often contain offensive language, and may not reflect how Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people identified themselves or their families.

### Maori (New Zealand)
Maori genealogy (whakapapa) is a living oral tradition maintained by iwi (tribes) and whanau (families). Whakapapa is considered taonga (a treasure) and is not always recorded in written form.

Written records that supplement oral tradition include:

- **Native Land Court minute books** (from 1862): Record whakapapa presented to the court to establish land claims. These are among the most detailed written records of Maori genealogy. Held by Archives New Zealand and increasingly digitized.
- **Church records**: Maori baptisms, marriages, and burials recorded by missionaries.
- **Census records**: Maori census records exist from the mid-1800s.
- **Te Ara**: https://teara.govt.nz/ provides cultural and historical context for Maori genealogical research.

Researchers unfamiliar with Maori culture should approach whakapapa research with respect and, where possible, in consultation with the relevant iwi.

## Naming Conventions

### European Settlers
Standard English naming conventions for the majority of records. Immigrants from non-English-speaking countries often had their names anglicized or misspelled in colonial records.

### Chinese Immigrants
Chinese immigrants' names were frequently mangled in colonial records. Transliteration was inconsistent, and many records list only a single name or a distorted version. The same person may appear under very different spellings in different documents.

### Indigenous Names
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people were often assigned European names by missionaries or government officials. Original indigenous names may not appear in official records, or may appear only as secondary entries.

Maori names in official records may use English baptismal names alongside Maori names. Both forms should be searched.

## Search Strategy (Australia)

1. Start with state BDM indexes to locate birth, marriage, and death records for the relevant state
2. Search Trove for newspaper mentions: obituaries, birth and marriage notices, court reports, shipping arrivals, and local news
3. Search the NAA RecordSearch database for immigration, military, and naturalization records
4. For convict ancestors, search Convict Records (https://convictrecords.com.au/) and LINC Tasmania
5. Search electoral rolls on Ancestry.com.au for addresses and occupations between census years
6. Check FamilySearch for church records and additional indexed collections
7. For post-WWII migrants, search NAA immigration case files

## Search Strategy (New Zealand)

1. Start with BDM Online indexes for births, marriages, and deaths
2. Search Papers Past for newspaper mentions: obituaries, shipping news, court reports, and family notices
3. Search Archives New Zealand (Archway catalog) for immigration, military, and naturalization records
4. Check FamilySearch for church records and additional indexed collections
5. For Maori ancestry, consult iwi records and Native Land Court minute books at Archives New Zealand

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not using Trove.** Trove is free and extraordinary. Millions of pages of Australian newspapers are searchable by keyword. Obituaries, family notices, shipping arrivals, court reports, and local news can fill gaps that no other source covers. It is the single most underused resource in Australian genealogy.
- **Assuming Australian records start in 1788.** Different states were colonized at different times: NSW in 1788, Tasmania in 1803, Western Australia in 1829, South Australia in 1836, Victoria in 1851 (separated from NSW), Queensland in 1859 (separated from NSW). Records for each state begin at different dates.
- **Privacy restrictions on recent vital records.** Each state restricts access to recent records. Typical restrictions: births (100 years), marriages (75 years), deaths (30 years), though these vary by state. Older records outside the restriction period are available through the historical indexes.
- **Treating convict ancestry as shameful.** Convict records are a rich genealogical source embraced by most Australian researchers. A convict ancestor often has more documented detail than a free settler from the same period because the colonial government tracked convicts so thoroughly.
- **New Zealand BDM certificates cost money.** The BDM Online index is free to search, but ordering a certificate costs NZD $33. Search the index first to confirm the correct entry, then order the certificate for full details.
- **Not checking Papers Past for New Zealand research.** Papers Past is the New Zealand equivalent of Trove. Free, full-text searchable, and covering newspapers from 1839 onward. It is the best free resource for supplementary New Zealand genealogical information.
- **Overlooking electoral rolls.** Because of mandatory voting, Australian electoral rolls from 1903 onward list nearly every adult with name, address, and occupation. They are uniquely complete compared to electoral records in other countries and provide regular snapshots of the population.
- **Ignoring state differences.** Australian genealogy is state-based. Records, archives, access rules, and digitization levels differ by state. A search strategy that works in NSW may not work in Western Australia. Always check the relevant state archive and BDM registry.
