# Oral History Protocol

How to collect, record, and incorporate family oral history into your research vault.

## Why Oral History Matters

Oral history is the most perishable data source in genealogy. When an elderly relative dies, their memories of family stories, names, places, and events are lost permanently. No archive or database can replace them.

Oral history is a tertiary source (it should be verified against documentary evidence), but it is an irreplaceable source of leads, context, and human detail.

## Before the Interview

### Prepare
1. Review your family tree and identify gaps that this person might fill
2. Prepare specific questions (see Question Templates below)
3. Bring photos, documents, or the family tree to prompt memories
4. Test your recording equipment

### Logistics
- Record the conversation (audio or video, with permission)
- Take brief notes during the interview for follow-up questions
- Plan for 60 to 90 minutes; elderly relatives tire easily but often have more to say than expected
- Choose a quiet, comfortable location

## During the Interview

### Start Broad, Then Focus
- Begin with open-ended questions: "Tell me about your parents"
- Let them talk. Do not interrupt stories, even if they seem tangential.
- Follow up with specific questions about names, dates, places
- Show photos or documents and ask "Who is this?" or "Where was this?"

### Question Templates

**For each generation back:**
- What was [ancestor]'s full name? Did they go by a nickname?
- Where were they born? Where did they grow up?
- What did they do for a living?
- Who did they marry? When and where?
- How many children did they have? What were their names?
- Where are they buried?
- What stories do you remember about them?

**For immigrant ancestors:**
- Where in [country] did they come from? (City? Village? Region?)
- When did they come to the US? Do you know the ship name?
- Why did they leave? (Economic, political, religious reasons?)
- Did they come alone or with family?
- Did they keep in touch with relatives in the old country?
- Did they speak English or keep speaking [language]?

**For context and leads:**
- Do you have any old photos, letters, or documents?
- Is there a family bible with records?
- Has anyone else in the family done genealogy research?
- Are there family stories that have been passed down? (Even ones that seem like legends)
- Is there anything about the family history that was never talked about?

## After the Interview

### Transcribe
- Create a transcription note using `templates/transcription.md`
- Note: document_type should be "oral-history"
- Include the date, interviewee, and interviewer in the frontmatter

### Extract Facts
- Pull every name, date, place, and relationship into the Extracted Facts table
- Mark confidence as "Low" for all oral history facts (pending verification)
- Note which statements are firsthand knowledge vs passed-down stories

### Integrate Into Vault
- Add leads to Open_Questions.md
- Add names to Unresolved_Persons.md (if not yet identified)
- Update person files with oral history details (marked as "[oral history, unverified]")
- Log the interview in Research_Log.md

### Verify
- Cross-reference oral history claims against documentary evidence
- If verified, upgrade the confidence level
- If contradicted, note the discrepancy but preserve the oral history version (it may contain partial truth)
