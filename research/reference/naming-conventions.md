# Naming Conventions

A guide to historical naming systems encountered in genealogy research.

## Patronymics (Scandinavian)

Used throughout Scandinavia until fixed surnames were adopted (Norway: law of 1923; Sweden: law of 1901; Denmark: law of 1828).

**How it works**: A child's surname is derived from the father's first name.
- Sons: [Father's name] + "sen" / "son" (Rasmusen, Andersson)
- Daughters: [Father's name] + "datter" / "dotter" (Rasmusdatter, Andersdotter)

**Implication**: Surnames change every generation. Paul, son of Anton, is "Paul Antonsen." Paul's son Rasmus is "Rasmus Paulsen" (not "Rasmus Antonsen").

**Search tip**: When searching for a pre-1900 Scandinavian ancestor, you need the father's first name to predict the patronymic. If you know "Rasmus Paulsen," his father was likely named Paul.

## Farm Names (Norwegian)

In Norway, people were also identified by the farm where they lived.

**How it works**: "[Name] [Farm Name]" identifies a person by their residence. "Paul Lillehaug" means "Paul who lives at the Lillehaug farm."

**Key points**:
- Moving to a new farm meant taking a new name
- The same person may appear under different farm names in different records
- Farm names became fixed surnames during emigration (immigrants chose a farm name as their permanent surname)
- Sub-farms (husmannsplasser) may not appear in standard farm indexes

## Przydomki (Polish Hereditary Nicknames)

A system unique to Poland where families with common surnames used hereditary nicknames to distinguish themselves.

**How it works**: A family named Kowalski (the 2nd most common Polish surname) might be known in their village as "Kowalski called Prusak." The przydomek ("Prusak") was passed from parent to child.

**Key points**:
- Przydomki appear in parish records: "Jan Prusak Kowalski" or "Jan vulgo Prusak"
- Upon emigration, some families adopted the przydomek as their American surname (because it was more distinctive than Kowalski)
- The przydomek itself may have meaning: "Prusak" = "the Prussian one" (possibly indicating origin from a Prussian-influenced region)

## German Naming

German surnames became fixed earlier than Scandinavian ones (generally by 1600 to 1700).

**Common patterns**:
- Occupational: Müller (miller), Schmidt (smith), Schneider (tailor)
- Geographic: Berliner (from Berlin), Wiener (from Vienna)
- Descriptive: Klein (small), Schwarz (black/dark)
- Patronymic (older): Heinrich (son of a Heinrich)

**Military names**: Men conscripted into the Swedish army (and some German armies) were assigned unique names to avoid confusion. These were often descriptive: Modig (brave), Stolt (proud), Ljung (heather). These became permanent surnames.

## Americanization Patterns

When immigrants arrived in the US, names were frequently changed:

| Pattern | Example | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Translation | Johannes → John | Direct translation of first name |
| Phonetic simplification | Lillehauge → Lillehaugen → Lillehaug | Dropped unfamiliar endings |
| Patronymic to surname | Johannesen → Johnson | Patronymic became permanent |
| Farm name to surname | [Farm Name] kept as family name | Common for Norwegian immigrants |
| Przydomek to surname | Kowalski → Prusak | Distinctive nickname replaced common surname |
| Official misspelling | Immigration officer wrote what they heard | Often permanent; family adopted the new spelling |
| Deliberate change | Completely new name | Sometimes for assimilation; sometimes legally changed |

**Search tip**: Always search for ALL known variants of a name. The passenger manifest spelling may be very different from the name used in America.

## Women's Names

Women's names in historical records follow different conventions than men's:

| Record Type | How Women Appear |
|---|---|
| Birth record | Maiden name (father's surname) |
| Marriage record | Maiden name → married name |
| Census | Married name (husband's surname) |
| Death certificate | Married name; maiden name in "mother's maiden name" field |
| Probate / will | May use "widow of [husband]" |
| Second marriage | Second husband's surname; maiden name may be lost |

**Search tip**: When searching for a woman, try maiden name, all married names, and "Mrs. [Husband's First] [Husband's Surname]" as search variants.
