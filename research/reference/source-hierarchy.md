# Source Hierarchy

When genealogical sources disagree, use this hierarchy to determine which source is more likely correct.

## The Hierarchy

### Tier 1: Primary Sources (created at or near the time of the event by an official authority)

- Vital records (birth, marriage, death certificates)
- Church registers (baptism, confirmation, marriage, burial)
- Military records (enlistment, discharge, service records)
- Immigration records (ship manifests, naturalization petitions)
- Census records (original enumeration sheets)
- Court records (probate, land, wills)
- Tax records and property records

**Why most reliable**: Created by an official or authorized person, usually close to the time of the event. The informant was often a direct participant.

**Caveat**: Even primary sources contain errors. A birth certificate filed 60 years after the birth may contain the informant's memory errors. A census taker may have misspelled a name. Always note the information's freshness relative to the event.

### Tier 2: Secondary Sources (compiled from primary sources by researchers, journalists, or publishers)

- Newspaper articles (obituaries, wedding announcements, biographical sketches)
- Published genealogies and county histories
- Compiled indexes (FamilySearch indexes, cemetery transcription projects)
- Historical society publications
- Digitized record summaries (as opposed to original images)

**Why less reliable**: The compiler may have misread, misinterpreted, or selectively edited the original source. Obituaries often contain errors in dates and relationships. Published genealogies may perpetuate earlier mistakes.

**Caveat**: High-quality secondary sources (peer-reviewed genealogies, well-documented county histories) can be quite reliable. Evaluate each source on its specific merits.

### Tier 3: Tertiary Sources (compiled from secondary sources or from memory)

- User-contributed family trees (Ancestry, Geni, WikiTree, MyHeritage)
- Oral history and family stories
- Photo captions and album labels
- Family tree software exports from unknown compilers
- Forum posts and genealogy blog entries

**Why least reliable**: Multiple layers of interpretation separate these from the original event. User-contributed trees frequently contain errors, unsourced claims, and copy-paste mistakes from other users' trees. Oral history conflates, compresses, and embellishes across generations.

**Caveat**: Oral history and family trees can point you toward the right records. They are valuable as leads, not as evidence. Always verify with Tier 1 or Tier 2 sources.

## When Sources Conflict: Decision Framework

1. **Compare tier levels.** A Tier 1 source outranks Tier 2, which outranks Tier 3.

2. **If sources are the same tier**, consider:
   - **Proximity to the event**: A record created at the time of the event is more reliable than one created decades later
   - **Informant knowledge**: Who provided the information? A parent registering a birth knows the facts; a distant relative providing information for a delayed certificate may not
   - **Internal consistency**: Does the source contradict itself?
   - **Corroboration**: Does any other independent source support one version over the other?

3. **If you still cannot resolve the conflict**:
   - Document both versions in the person file under `## Data Discrepancies`
   - Note which source you consider more likely correct and why
   - Create an Open Question entry if the discrepancy is significant
   - Do NOT silently choose one version

## The Two Independent Sources Standard

**Rule of thumb**: A genealogical claim should be supported by at least two independent sources before it is considered established.

"Independent" means the sources were created separately, not derived from each other. Two Ancestry trees that copied the same unverified data count as one source, not two.

**When one source is sufficient**:
- A primary source with no contradicting evidence (e.g., a birth certificate with a clear date)
- A well-documented secondary source (e.g., a peer-reviewed genealogy journal article)

**When more than two sources are needed**:
- Conflicting primary sources (rare but possible)
- Claims that significantly change the family tree structure
- Ethnic or religious identity assignments based on circumstantial evidence

## Common Genealogy Source Traps

- **Ancestry hints**: These are algorithmic matches, not verified connections. Always review the original record.
- **Geni tree merges**: Geni's collaborative model means anyone can edit anyone's tree. Edits may introduce errors.
- **"Same name, same dates" fallacy**: Two people with the same name born in the same year are not necessarily the same person, especially with common names.
- **Transcription errors propagating**: One misread date on a headstone gets transcribed into Find a Grave, then copied into Ancestry trees, then cited by dozens of researchers, giving a false impression of consensus.
- **Census age rounding**: People often rounded their ages on census forms. An age of "30" in 1900 does not necessarily mean birth year 1870.
