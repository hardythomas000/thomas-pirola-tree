# Glossary

Genealogy and genetic genealogy terms, explained for AI/tech practitioners and newcomers.

## Genealogy Terms

**Ahnentafel**: A numbering system for ancestors. You are #1, your father is #2, your mother is #3, paternal grandfather #4, etc. Any person's number times 2 gives their father; times 2 plus 1 gives their mother.

**Bygdebok** (Norwegian): A local history book covering a specific parish or district. Contains farm histories, family lineages, and community records. Often spans centuries. The most valuable single source for Norwegian genealogy.

**Census**: Government enumeration of the population. US censuses occur every 10 years (1790 to present). Norwegian censuses: 1801, 1865, 1875, 1891, 1900, 1910.

**Church books / Kirkebøker** (Norwegian) / **Kirchenbücher** (German): Parish registers recording baptisms, confirmations, marriages, and burials. The primary genealogical source before civil registration.

**Confidence tier**: A classification of how well-supported a genealogical claim is: Strong Signal, Moderate Signal, or Speculative.

**Delayed certificate**: A vital record (usually a birth certificate) filed years or decades after the event. Common for births before mandatory registration. Less reliable than timely filings due to informant memory errors.

**Farm name**: In Norwegian genealogy, a person's identifier based on the farm where they live. Not a hereditary surname. See Naming Conventions guide.

**GEDCOM**: GEnealogical Data COMmunication. The standard file format for exchanging family tree data between genealogy software programs. Version 5.5.1 is most widely used.

**Haplogroup**: A genetic population group defined by shared mutations in mitochondrial DNA (maternal) or Y-chromosome DNA (paternal). Used to trace deep ancestry (thousands of years).

**Husmannsplass** (Norwegian): A smallholding or sub-farm. Husmannsplasser do not always appear in standard farm name indexes, making their residents harder to find in records.

**Informant**: The person who provided information on a vital record (birth, death certificate). Identifying the informant helps assess reliability: a parent reporting a birth is more reliable than a distant relative reporting a death date from memory.

**Kurrent**: A historical German handwriting style used from the 16th to 20th centuries. Significantly different from modern handwriting and difficult for both humans and OCR software to read.

**Matricula**: Catholic parish records (from Latin "matricula" = register). Matricula Online is a free database of digitized Catholic parish records from Austria, Germany, Poland, and other countries.

**Ortsfamilienbuch** (German, plural: Ortsfamilienbücher): A village family book. A compiled genealogy covering every family in a specific village or parish, usually spanning several centuries. The German equivalent of a Norwegian bygdebok.

**Patronymic**: A naming system where the surname is derived from the father's first name. Rasmus Paulsen = Rasmus, son of Paul. Surnames change every generation.

**Primary source**: A record created at or near the time of the event by an authoritative person (e.g., a birth certificate, a church baptism register, a military discharge).

**Przydomek** (Polish, plural: przydomki): A hereditary family nickname used to distinguish families sharing a common legal surname. "Jan Prusak Kowalski" = Jan of the Kowalski family known as Prusak.

**Secundary source**: A record compiled from primary sources by a researcher, journalist, or publisher (e.g., a published county history, an obituary, a compiled index).

**Soundex**: A phonetic coding system that encodes surnames by sound rather than exact spelling. Used to index US census records. Helps find families even when names are misspelled.

**Tertiary source**: A record compiled from secondary sources or from memory (e.g., a user-contributed family tree, oral history, photo captions).

## Genetic Genealogy Terms

**Admixture**: The mixing of DNA from different populations through intermarriage. Admixture calculators estimate the proportions of different ancestral populations in your genome.

**Autosomal DNA (atDNA)**: DNA from the 22 non-sex chromosomes. Inherited from both parents and all ancestral lines. The basis for ethnicity estimates and DNA relative matching.

**centiMorgan (cM)**: A unit measuring the length of a DNA segment. Shared segments longer than 7 cM are likely inherited from a common ancestor. Segments shorter than 5 cM may be coincidental (identical by state rather than by descent).

**Chromosome painting**: A visualization showing which ancestry population each segment of each chromosome belongs to. Each chromosome has two copies (one from each parent).

**Endogamy**: Marriage within a closed community over many generations. Common in Ashkenazi Jewish, Amish, and some island populations. Creates unusually long shared DNA segments between distantly related individuals.

**GEDmatch**: A free third-party platform where you can upload raw DNA data from any testing company and run additional analyses (admixture calculators, one-to-many matching).

**mtDNA (mitochondrial DNA)**: DNA inherited exclusively through the maternal line (mother to all children). Used to trace the direct female line back thousands of years.

**Phasing**: The process of determining which DNA segments came from which parent. Can be done computationally (less accurate) or by testing a parent (definitive).

**Reference panel**: The set of individuals with known ancestry that a DNA testing company uses to compare your DNA against. Larger and more diverse panels produce better ethnicity estimates.

**SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism)**: A variation at a single position in the DNA sequence. Consumer DNA tests analyze 600K to 700K SNPs to estimate ancestry and health traits.

**Y-DNA**: DNA from the Y chromosome, inherited exclusively through the paternal line (father to sons only). Used to trace the direct male line back thousands of years.
