# DNA Interpretation Guardrails

A framework for responsibly interpreting consumer DNA test results in the context of genealogical research.

## What Can Be Confidently Inferred

### 1. Continental-Level Composition Is Reliable

If your DNA test says you are 99% European, that is near-certain. Broad continental categories (European, African, East Asian, etc.) are the most reliable output of consumer DNA tests because the genetic differences between continental populations are large and well-characterized.

### 2. Some Sub-Populations Are Very Distinctive

Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry is among the most reliably detected sub-populations. Due to a well-documented historical bottleneck and centuries of relative endogamy, Ashkenazi Jews have a distinctive genetic signature that all major testing companies detect accurately. If two providers agree on an Ashkenazi percentage within 1%, that signal is real.

Other well-resolved populations include: Finnish, Sardinian, and some Native American groups.

### 3. Haplogroups Confirm Deep Ancestry Lines

- **Paternal haplogroup** (Y-DNA): Traces the unbroken father-to-father line. Definitively regional at the macro level (e.g., R1a = Eastern European/Slavic; R1b = Western European; I1 = Scandinavian).
- **Maternal haplogroup** (mtDNA): Traces the unbroken mother-to-mother line. Less regionally specific than Y-DNA but still informative.

Haplogroups are deep ancestry markers (thousands of years) and should not be confused with recent genealogical ancestry.

### 4. Chromosome Painting Can Separate Parental Contributions

If your test provides per-chromosome ancestry data (23andMe's "Ancestry Composition" CSV), you receive two "copies" per chromosome (one from each parent). Analyzing which copy carries which ancestry can help separate parental contributions even without a parent's test, though this is probabilistic.

## What Should Be Inferred Cautiously

### 1. Specific Percentages Are Estimates, Not Measurements

The difference between 47.7% and 50% Scandinavian is within the margin of error. Treat percentages as ranges: "roughly half Scandinavian" is more honest than "47.7% Scandinavian."

### 2. Regional Matches Are Approximate

When 23andMe flags a specific county or MyHeritage highlights a region, these reflect statistical similarity to reference populations in those areas. Your ancestors may have lived in an adjacent county, or the genetic "boundaries" of the reference population may not align with political boundaries.

### 3. Assigning Genetic Components to Ancestor Lines Requires Genealogical Evidence

Saying "the 8% Ashkenazi comes from the [SURNAME] line" is a hypothesis, not a genetic proof. Without phased data or a parent's test, you cannot definitively assign segments to specific ancestors. The assignment becomes more confident when:
- Genealogical evidence independently predicts the genetic result
- The magnitude is consistent (e.g., 8% is plausible for one great-great-grandparent)
- No alternative ancestor line could explain the same signal

### 4. Provider Categorization Differences Are Real

One provider might report 22% "Eastern European" while another says 12% "Baltic." These are not contradictory results. They reflect different reference panels and different boundary decisions. The underlying DNA is the same; the labels differ.

### 5. Algorithm Updates Change Results

Your DNA does not change, but the interpretation does. Reference panels expand, algorithms improve, and previously "Broadly European" segments get reclassified. Treat current estimates as the best available interpretation, not the final word.

## What Should NOT Be Inferred

### 1. Percentages Do Not Directly Equal Ancestor Fractions

You do not inherit exactly 25% from each grandparent. Due to recombination, actual inheritance ranges from roughly 18% to 32% per grandparent, with wider variation at earlier generations. A 12.5% genetic component does not necessarily mean one great-grandparent was 100% that ancestry.

### 2. Trace Ancestry (<1%) Should Not Be Taken Seriously

Percentages below 1% are within the noise floor of consumer DNA tests. Do not build genealogical hypotheses on trace ancestry.

### 3. Small Components May Reflect Ancient Admixture, Not Recent Ancestors

A 2 to 3% Finnish component in a primarily Scandinavian genome likely reflects ancient Finno-Ugric admixture common across Scandinavia, not a specific Finnish ancestor. Similarly, small amounts of "Western Asian" in an Ashkenazi result reflect the Middle Eastern component of Ashkenazi ancestry, not a separate ancestor from the Middle East.

### 4. Haplogroups Describe ONE Lineage Each

Your paternal haplogroup traces only your father's father's father's... line. Your maternal haplogroup traces only your mother's mother's mother's... line. Together, they represent two out of potentially thousands of ancestral lineages. They say nothing about the vast majority of your ancestors.

### 5. DNA Cannot Determine Religion, Nationality, or Ethnicity

DNA reflects population genetics, not identity. A person with 100% "Eastern European" DNA could be Polish, Ukrainian, Russian, Lithuanian, or from any number of communities. "Ashkenazi Jewish" is a genetic population, not a religious determination. Use DNA as one line of evidence alongside documentary research.

## The Assignment Framework

A genetic component should only be "assigned" to a specific ancestor line when ALL of the following are true:

1. **Genealogical evidence independently predicts it.** Documented ancestors from that region or population exist in the tree.
2. **The magnitude is plausible.** The percentage is consistent with the ancestor's position in the tree (e.g., a great-great-grandparent contributing roughly 6 to 12%).
3. **No alternative explanation exists.** If multiple ancestor lines could explain the signal, you cannot assign it to just one without additional evidence.
4. **Ideally, phased data confirms it.** Seeing which parent contributed which chromosome segments is the strongest form of assignment. Without phasing, assignment is probabilistic.

## Provider Bias Notes

- **23andMe** tends to have better Scandinavian resolution and more specific regional matches within Europe
- **MyHeritage** tends to over-report broad categories like "North and West European" as catch-all buckets
- **AncestryDNA** has the largest database for DNA matching but ethnicity estimates can shift significantly between updates
- **All providers** have stronger reference panels for well-studied populations (Europeans, East Asians) and weaker panels for underrepresented populations

## Haplogroup Limitations

- Haplogroups are inherited through a single line (patrilineal or matrilineal) and represent a tiny fraction of your total ancestry
- They are useful for confirming broad geographic origins but not for recent genealogy
- Two people sharing a haplogroup may share a common ancestor thousands of years ago, not a genealogically relevant one
- Subclade resolution varies by testing platform; some provide deep subclades while others give only the macro haplogroup
