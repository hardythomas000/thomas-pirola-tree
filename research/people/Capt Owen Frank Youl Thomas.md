## Notes

One of Qantas's earliest pilots. WWII: civilian pilot ferrying people to/from occupied Singapore in a float plane. Commanded Avro Lancastrian G-AGLX — disappeared over Indian Ocean 23 Mar 1946. 10 on board, never found.

## Detailed Research (compiled Mar 2026)

### Career with Qantas Empire Airways

Captain Frank Thomas was one of Qantas's most experienced Indian Ocean service pilots. The Indian Ocean route — between Western Australia and Ceylon (Sri Lanka) — was the world's longest regular civil aviation nonstop route at 3,600+ statute miles. Aircraft maintained radio silence and navigated by astro navigation and dead reckoning.

- **By Nov 1944**: Had made 20+ round flights in Catalinas and Liberators between WA and Ceylon/Karachi
- **Service began**: July 1943, with RAF assistance (was on the secret list until a few months before Nov 1944)
- **Fellow Qantas captains on the route**: R.B. Tapp, L.R. Ambrose, D.F. MacMaster, J.L. Grey
- **Described as**: "veteran pilot of the Indian Ocean service" who "knows the West Australian coast well"
- **Crew normally of five** on these crossings

### Record-Breaking Flight — 8 November 1944

Captain Thomas set the record for the Indian Ocean civil air service by flying nonstop from Ceylon to Western Australia in **17½ hours** in a Qantas Liberator — 3,600 statute miles at an average speed of 200+ mph.

**VIP passenger**: Sir Ronald Cross, British High Commissioner to Australia, who had been visiting Admiral Lord Louis Mountbatten in Kandy, Ceylon, and Lt Gen Sir William Slim in Bengal.

Journalist Geoffrey Tebbutt, who was aboard, wrote for the Daily News (Perth):
> "Captain Thomas was up there peering below his long eyeshade for ways through or round the turbulent masses of cumulus."
> "conducted with the finest and most mature judgment of airmanship to which hardened experts are prepared to take off their hats."

The flight carried 2,900 gallons of fuel ("about 60 years' ration for a large car in Australia"). Route described as "Australia's most expert and imaginative achievement in civil aviation." (Source: Trove article 78765123)

### The Disappearance — 23 March 1946

**Aircraft**: Avro 691 Lancastrian C.1, registration G-AGLX, MSN 1178, Rolls-Royce Merlin engines. Originally ordered as Lancaster B Mk. III PD146, re-serialled VF165, civil registered 1 Dec 1944 to BOAC. Operated by Qantas, leased from BOAC.

**Route**: London – Karachi – Colombo – Cocos Islands – Perth – Sydney. BOAC operated London–Karachi; Qantas operated Karachi onwards.

**Timeline**:
- Departure from Negombo RAF Station, Colombo was **delayed 2 hours** due to a fault in the radio equipment
- Departed for Cocos (Keeling) Islands — 3,621 miles to Guildford (Perth) via Cocos
- Last position report at **18:00 Perth time** on Saturday 23 March (5 hours out from Colombo, ~1,600 miles from Colombo, about 3 hours from Cocos Island)
- Scheduled 18:30 report was never received
- Last reported position was north of Cocos Islands, in the inter-tropical area with the worst weather front on the route
- Registration cancelled 23 March 1946 as **"Lost at Sea"**

**Crew (5)**:
- **Capt. Frank Thomas** — commander
- **First Officer McClelland**
- **Navigating Officer Nuske**
- **Radio Officer McBean**
- **Flight Steward Porteous**

All five crew were from New South Wales; four had Sydney suburban addresses.

**Passengers (5)**:
- **Mr. Jack Dobson** — son of AVRO's Managing Director Roy H. (later Sir Roy) Dobson. Was travelling to Australia to help launch Avro Lincoln production.
- **Mr. J. Knight**
- **Mr. H. Marshland** (also recorded as R. Marsland)
- **Mr. J.H. Vose**
- **Mr. H.E. Wittey** (also recorded as H. Whittey)

All five passengers were booked from the United Kingdom to Sydney.

### The Search

- **Round-the-clock search** launched immediately — 15 aircraft from Ceylon, Cocos, Learmonth, Darwin and Java
- RAAF Liberator searched coast from Fremantle to Port Hedland — unavailing
- Air-sea rescue Catalina from Crawley Bay searched toward Cocos Island
- Special Lancastrian from Sydney piloted by **Captain L. Ambrose** (former Qantas operations manager)
- RAF planes from Cocos Island searched the vicinity
- MacRobertson-Miller Aviation dispatched DH-86 under Capt. J. Woods to Learmonth
- **Captain Thomas's pre-flight plan had Learmonth as alternative landfall** if bypassing Cocos
- **Faint undecipherable signal** picked up by searching RAF Liberator from Cocos shortly before dusk on 24 March — "such as could have originated from a dinghy transmitter"
- **5 April 1946**: Heliograph signals reportedly intercepted near **Beagle Bay Mission** (~90 miles north of Broome, WA). Qantas would neither confirm nor deny. (Source: Trove article 50334537)
- The plane carried **two dinghies** stocked with food, water, and heliograph equipment
- No trace of the aircraft was ever found

### Probate

Three probate/advertising notices appeared in newspapers:
- Sydney Morning Herald, 4 October 1946, Page 13 (Trove article 17997509)
- Sydney Morning Herald, 25 March 1947, Page 14 (Trove article 18019319)
- The Age, Melbourne, 1 May 1947, Page 9 — described as "late of Sydney, but formerly of Hillston, in the State of New South Wales" (Trove article 206025847)

The Hillston reference confirms his father Owen Thomas's connection to Hillston, NSW.

### Context: The "Double Sunrise" Service

The Indian Ocean service was famously known as the "Double Sunrise" route because the longest Catalina flights (28-33 hours) would see two sunrises in a single crossing. A book documenting this service: *Double Sunrise: Qantas Empire Airways Indian Ocean Wartime Services 1943-1946* by Geoff Goodall.
